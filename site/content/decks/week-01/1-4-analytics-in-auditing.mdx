<Slide title="Audit Data Analytics (ADA)">

- “the science and art of discovering and analyzing patterns; identifying anomalies; and extracting other useful information in data underlying or related to the subject matter of an audit through analysis, modeling, and visualization” ~ AICPA
- Identify and quantify risk
- Identify internal control deficiencies
- Search for errors, inconsistencies, or misstatements

</Slide>

<Slide title="AICPA Regulatory Guidance">

- SAS No. 142: Audit Evidence
  - Codifies use of ADAs in audits for risk assessment and substantive procedures, item identification, etc.
- Audit Data Standards (ADS)
  - Table and variable names for common processes
    - General Ledger, Order to Cash, Procure to Pay, Inventory
- Guide to Audit Data Analytics
  - Practical resource for real applications

</Slide>

<Slide title="PCAOB Regulatory Guidance">

- AS 1105: Audit Evidence
  - Addresses responsibilities when evaluating reliability of firm information used as audit evidence
- AS 2110 & 2301: on Risks of Misstatement
  - Addresses analytics to assess and respond to risks
- AS 2305: Substantive Analytical Procedures
  - Addresses the use of analytics as substantive tests
- AS 2810: Evaluating Audit Results
  - Addresses analytics as part of review stage

</Slide>

<Slide title="Sampling to Full-Population Testing">

- Historically, auditors relied on sampling to make conclusions about transactions
  - Sampling misses rare transactions
- Now auditors analyze entire population of transactions, eliminating sampling risk
- Observations are still examined, but based on selection criteria, called audit by exception (ABE)
  - General Ledger Analytics Tools implement this

</Slide>

<Slide title="Risk Assessment">

- Auditors perform risk assessment to understand the firm, its environment and internal controls, to assess risk of material misstatements
- Analytics and visualizations are used to fill in a more complete picture of the firm and its economics
  - Trend analysis
  - Clustering or subset analysis
- Used to focus auditor time and effort

</Slide>

<Slide title="Substantive Testing">

- Substantive procedures are used to detect material misstatements
  - Journal entry testing
  - Revenue recognition
  - Inventory testing

</Slide>

<Slide title="Data Breadth">

- Auditors now employ techniques allowing combination of multiple disparate data sources
- More data allows auditors to capture broader insight
- Example: comparing firm bank and credit card statements to payroll and CEO’s personal bank accounts to uncover extent of fraud

</Slide>

<Slide title="Internal Audit">

- External audit can leverage internal audit
  - Provide audit evidence and direct assistance (SAS 128 / AS 2605)
  - Streamline audit, better outcomes, reduced cost
- Internal audit also covers operational efficiency and strategic decision-making
- Continuous auditing
  - Automated tests and models run on data as it comes in

</Slide>

<Slide title="Internal Operational Audit Examples">

- Procure to pay cycle: Analyze POs, vendor invoices, and payments to find erroneous payments, unauthorized suppliers, split POs
- Order to cash cycle: Analyze sales orders and receivables to find unbilled deliveries, credit limit exceptions, collection delays
- Inventory management: Analyze inventory turnover to identify obsolete stock, optimize inventory levels

</Slide>

<Slide title="Caveats and Cautions">

- Adoption frictions (33% internal auditors w/o ADA)
- Lack of skills (49% of internal auditors)
- Data quality and model assumptions
- Perception and the “Effort Heuristic”
  - Reviewers judge tech assisted audits as lower quality
- Full population testing may create false security
  - Study found auditors overestimated internal data because they had tested it comprehensively
