<Slide title="What is Storytelling">

- A story is a set of data, observations, or events that are presented in a way or order intended to evoke a reaction or specific conclusion
- Can be as simple as well designed visualization
- Can be fully narrative, without tables or figures

<Slide title="Why use storytelling">

- Memorable format aids retention
- Customize message to audience
- Contextualizing analysis can improve interpretation
- Facilitate and/or prompt decision-making

</Slide>

<Slide title="How to tell stories">

- Start with main idea
  - What conclusion do you want your audience to draw?
- Consider audience and format, customize to match
- Find a hook – something to grab attention and anchor memory
- Storyboard – lay out the structure of your story
  - Slides, post-it notes, pen and paper. Flexibility is key.

</Slide>

<Slide title="Example – Variance Analysis">

- You are an in-house accountant presenting quarterly budget variance results to department heads.
- The budget variance is net unfavorable driven primarily by Marketing spending more than expected on a new product launch
- The goal is to explain why the company is over, not just that they are.

</Slide>

<Slide title="Example – Variance Analysis">

- Main Idea
- What conclusion do you want your audience to draw?
  - Identify the main issue (Marketing overspend)
  - Highlight the magnitude (15%)
  - Present primary causes (digital ads, contractors)
  - State the required action (review process)
- Example:
  - "While most departments managed spending effectively last quarter, the Marketing department's significant overspend (15% above budget) was primarily driven by unexpected costs related to the new product launch campaign, specifically digital advertising and contractor fees. We need to review campaign budgeting processes for future launches."

</Slide>

<Slide title="Example – Variance Analysis">

- Audience and Format
- Audience: Department heads.
  - They need clear, concise information relevant to their area and potential actions they need to take.
  - Keep information high level, non technical, though budgeting & variance should be familiar
- Format: 15 minute presentation
  - Slide deck with planned 4-8 slides
  - Have supporting information on backup slides if asked
  - Emphasize visuals and keep text concise

</Slide>

<Slide title="Example – Variance Analysis">

- Potential Hooks
- Direct: One department's spending variance is driving our overall budget performance this quarter. Let's dive into the Marketing story.
- Benefit-Oriented: Understanding our Q3 spending variances helps us optimize resource allocation for Q4. We'll focus today on the biggest learning opportunity: the Marketing campaign launch.
- Relatable: Marketing faced something that could happen to any department: unexpected headwinds during the product launch. Let's break down what happened and how we can better prepare next time.

</Slide>

<Slide title="Example – Variance Analysis">

- Storyboarding
- Slide 1 (Hook & Main Idea): Q3 Spending Analysis: Marketing’s Product Launch Variance.
- Slide 2 (Overall Picture): Chart showing Budget vs. Actual by department, green/red color coding to highlight Marketing as outlier
- Slide 3 (Marketing Deep Dive - The “Why”): Waterfall chart showing variances by category. Make the bars for "Digital Advertising" and "Contractor Fees" visually stand out as the largest negative variances consuming the budget.
- Slide 4 (Context): Timeline chart showing Marketing’s variances from previous period through launch period.
- Slide 5 (Recommendation): List clear, actionable recommendations (e.g., Implement phased budget approval for campaign milestones).

</Slide>
