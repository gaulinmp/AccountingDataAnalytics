<Slide title="Choosing Visualizations">

- Choosing the right visualization involves balancing:
  - What can you show? (Data type & structure)
  - What do you need to show? (Analytical goal)
  - Who are you showing it to? (Their needs & literacy)
  - How will they see it? (Medium & surrounding narrative)
- Start with the question, not the chart
- Prioritize clarity and accuracy
- Know your audience and tailor accordingly
- Iterate! Your first idea might not be the best one. Get feedback.

</Slide>

<Slide title="Considerations">

- Choosing the right visualization is about best conveying message and maximizing impact and retention
- Nature and type of data being analyzed
- Analytical question
- Intended audience
- Context

</Slide>

<Slide title="Nature and Type of Data">

- Continuous numeric (e.g. assets, price) or discrete numeric (# employees, # products)
  - Line chart, histograms, scatter plot, box plot, heat map
- Categorical nominal (e.g. products, regions) or ordinal (e.g. customer reviews, credit rating)
  - Bar charts, stacked bar charts, heat maps, pie chart, dot plot
- Text data: word cloud, topic model visualizations

</Slide>

<Slide title="Nature and Type of Data">

- Multiple variables
  - Scatter plots, x-y line chart, grouped bar chart, pair plot
  - Many variables: trace plots, heatmaps, bubble charts
- Time dimension
  - Line charts, area charts, gantt charts
- Spatial dimension
  - Maps, choropleth maps, heat maps, network graph

</Slide>

<Slide title="What is the Question">

- Comparing variables: scatter plot or x-y graph
- Understanding variance: histogram or density plot
- Understanding relationships: x-y graph, trend lines
- Understanding trends and projections: line graph, confidence intervals
- Understanding composition: stacked bar/area chart, treemap
- Telling a story: complex combinations, have fun!

</Slide>

<Slide title="Intended Audience">

- General Public: simple graphs, minimal messages, self-explanatory graphics (labels, arrows, etc)
  - Stick to familiar charts, line, bar, pie, etc.
- Executives: can handle complexity, but want actionable intelligence
  - Familiar charts, KPI indicators, dashboards
- Experts (practicing accountants): can handle complexity, but could get lost in details. Avoid superfluous information to keep focus, and provide accurate evidence and rigor

</Slide>

<Slide title="Intended Audience">

- Goal of audience
  - Exploratory analysis or “first look”: can provide more information, interactivity a plus
  - Decision making: concise graphics with decisions or alternatives highlighted (costs and benefits)
  - Historical context: clear delineation between past and present, highlighting similarities and deviations, changing assumptions or conditions
- Familiarity with subject allows for more complexity
- Time dimension: a brief look necessitates more simplicity than a long study

</Slide>

<Slide title="Context">

- Medium
  - Report (pdf, web, printed): should be self-explaining, clearly labeled, can have small features
  - Presentation: can explain more complexity, must be visible from a distance
  - Interactive dashboard: should be self-explaining, but can have more complexity that is understandable through interaction
- Narrative / story
  - Standalone: needs to be self-contained & self-explaining
  - Part of a story or report: can assume some knowledge on the audience’s part given where in the story/report is occurs

</Slide>
