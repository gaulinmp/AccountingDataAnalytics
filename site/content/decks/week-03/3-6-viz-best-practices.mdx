<Slide title="Best Practices">

- Informative
- Focus attention, convey primary point(s)
- Targeted to audience

</Slide>

<Slide title="Make it Informative">

- Visualization should clearly answer a specific question or communicate a key message
- Try for one point per visualization to avoid overload
- Ensure viewers can understand the point without needing extensive explanation (keep footnotes short!)
- Include all necessary information to convey the point
  - Use clear titles, axis labels (with units!), and legends that sufficiently describe what is being presented

</Slide>

<Slide title="Focus Attention">

- Guide your audience's eyes to the most critical information effortlessly
- Use visual clues
  - Color, size, →arrows←,( spacing ), ordering data
  - Motion/animation (if medium allows)
- Avoid overwhelming viewer
  - Limit number of focal points per graphic

</Slide>

<Slide title="Target your Audience">

- Design choices must align with who will be viewing the visualization and how they will be viewing it
- Consider audience’s knowledge of subject
  - Different level of complexity and jargon for accounting audience vs general public
- Consider audience’s data literacy
  - Will they understand distributions, trend-lines, etc?
- Is the graphic printed, on the web, mobile, etc?
  - Online distribution allows for interaction, mobile may have size/space limitations

</Slide>

<Slide title="Accounting Considerations">

- Provide context – Accounting numbers rarely stand alone
  - Last year’s numbers, historical trends, etc.
  - Peer firms, industry comparison, etc.
- Label public vs private
  - Information security is important at firms
- Specify audited vs non-audited numbers

</Slide>

<Slide title="Best Practices – Practical Considerations">

- Concise, simple
- Clean (avoid clutter and noise)
- Aesthetic
- Interesting

</Slide>

<Slide title="Concise and Simple">

- Make the main point clear and easy to consume
- The fewer main takeaways per visual the better for retention
- Remove unnecessary or redundant information
- Avoid jargon the audience will not understand

</Slide>

<Slide title="Clutter and Noise">

- Remove non-essential visual elements that distract from the data itself
- Increase “data-to-ink ratio”
- Complexity adds cost to interpretation, detracts from main point
- Eliminate distracting elements
  - Excessive gridlines, unnecessary borders, background images/textures, shadows, 3D effects, and other elements that don't aid comprehension
- Use whitespace effectively to separate elements and aid readability

</Slide>

<Slide title="Professional Aesthetics">

- A visually appealing, professional-looking chart enhances credibility and engagement
- Use a consistent style
  - Some software defaults are terrible (e.g. Stata)
- Consider color theory and branding

</Slide>

<Slide title="Interesting">

- Capture your audience's attention and make the data memorable
- Select visualizations that make an interesting point
  - Not all data or messages should be visualized
- Tell a story with your data
- Be creative and strive for something visually engaging

</Slide>
