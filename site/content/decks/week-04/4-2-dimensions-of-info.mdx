<Slide title="dimensionality">

- Dimensionality is the number of attributes, features, or variables recorded for each observation or data point
  - Length – 1 dimension
  - Area: length & width – 2 dimensions
  - Volume: length & width & height – 3 dimensions

</Slide>

<Slide title="Multidimensional data">

- Multiple measurements for single observation
  - Journal entry – date, account info, names, amount, etc.
  - Consumer – demographics, location, interactions
  - Financials
- Multidimensional due to computer representation
  - Location stored as latitude and longitude (& height?)
- Text? Images?

</Slide>

<Slide title="When Dimensions explode">

- Text stored as a numbers in computers
- Images are two dimensional (more with color, depth, etc.), but each pixel is a dimension in practice
- Vectors: list of numbers (x,y,z) that have some relation
  - In physics, vectors represent position, time, movement
  - In LLMs, all inputs are “vectorized” into embeddings
- Embeddings: encode information into dimensions and the relationship between them
  - Dimensions are abstract, not necessarily interpretable

</Slide>

<Slide title="Why do we care about dimensions?">

- Important to understand relationships between variables
  - Relationships between dimensions should be treated differently than relationships between observations
  - Be careful treating dimensions differently (e.g., standardizing width and not height)
- When dimensions get large (think text or images) we need some way to analyze them
  - Graphs can only represent up to three dimensions

</Slide>

<Slide title="Seeing beyond 2D">

- Visualization of three or more dimensions is difficult
  - 3D graphs can be hard to interpret without interactivity
  - Other features can be introduced (bubble size, color, etc.) but are less self-explanatory and more complex
- Interactive visualizations can help by giving audience control over what they viewing
  - Can swap between dimensions being observed
  - Can incorporate time / motion

</Slide>

<Slide title="Visualizing high dimensions">

- Faceting: Break the data into multiple small plots in a grid based on categories or other dimensions
  - Plot expenses over time by category in grid of states or country
- Dimensionality Reduction: Use algorithm (PCA, t-SNE, UMAP) to project high-dimensional data into fewer dimensions
  - Preserve structure or relationships (e.g., similar points in high-D stay close in 2D)
  - Always results in loss of information, and the new dimensions often lack direct interpretation

</Slide>

<Slide title="Conclusion">

- Understand data and its dimensions to properly treat observations
- Match visualization techniques to the dimensionality of your data
  - Visual features, grouping visualizations, dimension reduction
- There is no perfect solution
  - Visualizing high-D data always involves trade-offs (detail vs. overview, information loss vs. clarity)
  - The goal is insight, not perfect representation

</Slide>
