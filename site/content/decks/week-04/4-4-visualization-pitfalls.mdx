<Slide title="Caution">

- “There are 3 kinds of lies: lies, damned lies, and statistics.” ~ Mark Twain
- Visualizations can be misleading (intentionally or not)
- Important to consider whether the interpretation of your visualization will be what is intended

</Slide>

<Slide title="Common mistakes">

- Visual distortion
- Cherry-picking Data
  - Outlier treatment
- Misleading Aggregations
- Comparing Dissimilar Data

</Slide>

<Slide title="Visual Distortion">

- Truncation: Truncating axes can make variation appear greater or less
- Scaling: Scaling axes by non-constant amounts distorts visual comparison
  - Log-scale axes are an example of well-understood non-constant scaling
  - Twin axes: A second axis can be misleading if using same units as first

</Slide>

<Slide title="Cherry-Picking Data">

- Including only those observations that support a specific conclusion or omitting contradictory evidence
- Removing outliers without justification can present a cleaner, but potentially misleading, picture
- Selecting sub-group may imply the results generalize to the full group

</Slide>

<Slide title="Misleading Aggregation">

- Using the mean for highly skewed data can be misleading, where the median might be better
- Aggregating data can mask recent changes in trends

</Slide>

<Slide title="mean vs median">

- Mean (expected value) is weighted by values
- Median is “middle” of data
- Be careful to assess what you believe your audience will interpret each to mean

- Data: https://www.cbo.gov/publication/60706#_idTextAnchor039

</Slide>

<Slide title="Comparing Dissimilar Data">

- Comparing metrics that aren’t truly comparable (e.g., plotting levels and percentages on the same graph without clear labels)
- Plotting spurious correlations together to imply a relationship
  - e.g., tylervigen.com/spurious-correlations

</Slide>

<Slide title="Conclusion">

- As a producer
  - Avoid common pitfalls
  - Consider how yours or the audience’s biases or assumptions affect interpretation of the information
- As a consumer
  - Look for common pitfalls
  - Consider what information may be missing
  - Consider what assumptions underlie the data

</Slide>
