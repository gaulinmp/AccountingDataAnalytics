<Slide title="What is supervised Learning">

- Developing a model based on predicting something we already know
  - Diagnostic: understand why something happened
  - Predictive: what we know helps forecast the future
- Requires us to have data on the outcome that we wish to predict
  - This is called “labeled” data; the outcome is the “label”
- Label = f(Features)

</Slide>

<Slide title="Why focus on supervised learning?">

- It encompasses a lot of the modeling you may do
- It is a powerful tool for answering many questions
  - Forecasting future revenues/expenses/demand
  - Calculating cost functions
  - Identifying causality
- It comes with caveats and cautions to be aware of

</Slide>

<Slide title="Uses of Supervised Learning">

- Diagnostic modeling: understanding the why/how
  - Often interested in interpreting the model’s innards
- Predictive modeling: forecasting, counter-factuals
  - Often more interested in accuracy than interpretability

</Slide>

<Slide title="Categories of Supervised Learning">

- Regression: predicting continuous values
  - Example: estimating cost functions (fixed/variable cost)
- Classification: predicting categories
  - Example: categorizing transactions for general ledger

</Slide>

- Week 8: Modeling Overview 🤖

- The Data Modeling Lifecycle - An overview of the steps from defining the problem to deploying and monitoring a model.
- A Tour of Model Types: Descriptive to Prescriptive - Exploring the different categories of analytical models (descriptive, diagnostic, predictive, prescriptive) with accounting examples.
- Training vs. Testing: Preparing Your Data for Modeling - Explaining the importance of splitting data and how to do it.
- Key Modeling Concepts: Features, Labels, and Algorithms - Defining essential terminology in an accessible way.

- Week 9: Regression 📈
- This week dives into regression analysis, a fundamental predictive modeling technique used for forecasting and understanding relationships.
- Introduction to Regression: Predicting Continuous Values - Explaining the core concept of regression and its uses in accounting.
- Simple Linear Regression: The One-Variable Wonder - How to build and interpret a simple linear regression model (e.g., predicting costs based on activity).
- Running Simple Regression in Practice (Excel/Python/R) - A hands-on demonstration using a common tool.
- Multiple Linear Regression: Juggling Multiple Drivers - Expanding to models with several independent variables (e.g., predicting revenue based on marketing spend and economic indicators).

- Don't Break the Rules! Checking Regression Assumptions - Discussing linearity, independence, homoscedasticity, and normality.
