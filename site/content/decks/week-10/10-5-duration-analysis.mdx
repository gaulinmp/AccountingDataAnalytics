<Slide title="Duration Analysis">

- Many classifiers are “one shot”
- What about factors that build to a tipping point?
  - E.g., bankruptcy, M&A completion
- Duration analysis, survival analysis, hazard models
- Two components:
  - Discrete: did it happen
  - Continuous: when did it happen

</Slide>

<Slide title="Example: Loan Default">

- Syndicated loan is instantiated
- Borrower must adhere to covenants, tested regularly
- Default on violated covenant or missed payments
- Over life of loan, outcome either default or term ends
- Prediction: will borrower default and when?

</Slide>

<Slide title="Censoring">

- Right censoring is when the outcome is not observed
  - E.g., loan term ends before default
- Left censoring is when outcome has already occurred
  - E.g., firm already in default when observation starts
- Interval censoring is when outcome occurs in some range, or window
  - E.g., default only measured quarterly

</Slide>

<Slide title="Purpose of Duration Analysis">

- Identify what correlates with “survival”
  - Whether a variable relates to the outcome occurring
  - How much that variable effects outcome probability
- Measure effect on probability (more likely ever)
- Measure effect on time to failure (more likely sooner)

</Slide>

<Slide title="Cautions and Caveats">

- Model fit depends on amount of time-series data
- Model assumptions can be strong
  - Non-parametric models are flexible but need more data
  - Parametric models are less flexible but more potential for deep insight
  - In the middle: Cox proportional model

</Slide>
