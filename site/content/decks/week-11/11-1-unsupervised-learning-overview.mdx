<Slide title="Why unsupervised?">

- Supervised learning is great at predicting what we want to know
- What if we don’t know what we don’t know?
  - Are there customer “types” that we should be aware of
  - What information is in text disclosures?

</Slide>

<Slide title="Analogy – Sorting Skittles">

- A bag of skittles is dumped on the floor, and all measurements taken (color, size, weight, etc.)
- Clustering – separate skittles into colors
  - A regression approach might know that lime and green-apple are different, clustering might not
- Dimensionality Reduction – strongest “factor” is color, then weight, no difference in size

</Slide>

<Slide title="unsupervised Learning">

- Used when we don’t have outcomes
- Approaches capture structures and relationships
- Example: identifying groups, computing factors

<ImageCaption src="/decks/week-11/11-1-unsupervised-learning-overview/img2.png" alt="unsupervised Learning" />

</Slide>

<Slide title="Clustering in Accounting">

- Example: grouping transactions into types
  - Classification might predict which account applies
  - Clustering might find non directly labeled clusters such as “discretionary”, “periodic”, “abnormal”, etc.
  - Clusters could capture factors that span accounts, but have similar features that may be worth monitoring

</Slide>

<Slide title="Dimensionality Reduction in Accounting">

- Example: deriving customers risk “factors”
  - Regression might predict probability of bad debt
  - Dimensionality reduction might find risk “factors”, such as financial stability, risk appetite, loyalty, etc.
  - Factors aid in understanding customer types, such as the stable risk seeker, or safe loyal customer, and can be used to decide services provided or AR terms

</Slide>

<Slide title="Cautions and Caveats">

- Often require a lot of data
- Need careful consideration of out-of-sample validity
- Clusters may not have business application

</Slide>
