<Slide title="What is clustering?">

- Supervised learning is great at predicting what we want to know
- What if we don’t know what we don’t know?
  - Are there customer “types” that we should be aware of
  - What information is in text disclosures?

</Slide>

<Slide title="General approaches">

- Centroid approach – clusters have “centers” and your assigned cluster is the closest one
- Density approach – clusters are concentrations of observations, like cities are densely populated areas
- Hierarchical approach – clusters form a hierarchy, like a tree diagram

</Slide>

<Slide title="Centroid Workhorse – K-means">

- Algorithm:
  - Choose K random cluster centers, assign every point to its nearest cluster
  - Recompute the center of each cluster as center of its points, re-assign clusters to each point
  - Repeat #2 until there is no more movement
- Choosing K and the first random centers both can have huge impacts on outcome

</Slide>

<Slide title="Density Clustering">

- Connects areas of “high density” into clusters
- Discovers # of clusters
- Outliers in no cluster (good for anomaly detection)
- Like cities, hard to draw boundaries, hard to compare NYC and Moab

<ImageCaption src="/decks/week-11/11-2-clustering/img4.png" alt="Density Clustering" />

</Slide>

<Slide title="Hierarchical Clustering">

- Determines a hierarchy by:
  - Splitting dataset – top down
  - Combining points – bottom up
- A cluster distance can be set to get N clusters at that point
  - Smaller distance  more clusters
- Good for hierarchical data

</Slide>

<Slide title="Cautions and Caveats">

- Lots of accounting and finance data are clustered due to their hierarchical nature (e.g. sub-accounts)
  - This requires special consideration so the clustering algorithm does not just re-discover the hierarchy
- Clusters may not “converge” and just be noise
- Clusters are not always interpretable
- Clusters may not have business application

</Slide>
